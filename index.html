01 — The Landscape

What Small Dealer Exits Actually Look Like

Most golf cart dealers are owner-operated businesses. That creates both the value and the biggest challenge when it comes time to sell.

The golf cart and low-speed vehicle market has grown significantly over the past several years, driven by planned communities, resort areas, and the broader shift toward neighborhood-scale electric transportation. Manufacturers like ICON EV (founded in Tampa in 2017) have disrupted the market by offering premium features at accessible price points, which has made the dealer model more attractive to buyers who see growth potential in this segment.

However, selling a small dealership is fundamentally different from selling a franchise auto dealer. There are no "blue sky" franchise multiples to lean on. Your value lives in your customer relationships, your inventory pipeline, your rental fleet, your local reputation, and — critically — whether the business can run without you standing behind the counter.

What is SDE?

Seller's Discretionary Earnings is the most important number in small business valuation. It's your net income plus your salary, plus any personal expenses you run through the business (vehicle, phone, insurance), plus any one-time or non-recurring expenses. It answers the question: "How much cash does this business actually put in the owner's pocket?"

For a dealer doing 100+ units per year with a rental operation, you're likely looking at a business with meaningful value — but only if a buyer can verify the numbers and see a path to running it themselves. That's what the rest of this guide is about.

02 — Valuation

What Is Your Business Actually Worth?

The honest answer is: it depends entirely on how well you can document what you've built and whether a buyer can see themselves stepping into your shoes.

The Math Buyers Will Run

Every serious buyer is going to work backwards from one number: how much cash this business generates for the owner. They'll take your SDE and apply a multiple based on risk, growth potential, and how dependent the operation is on you personally.

The Owner-Dependency Discount

If the business can't function without you — if all the customer relationships, vendor contacts, and rental scheduling live in your head or on paper — buyers will discount the value significantly. They're not buying a business; they're buying a job. The single biggest lever you have to increase sale price is making the business transferable.

What Adds Value Beyond the Numbers

Beyond raw earnings, buyers pay premiums for things that reduce their risk and increase their confidence:

Dealer relationships — An authorized ICON dealership carries weight. If that relationship is formalized and transferable, it's a real asset. Rental fleet with history — A fleet of 10-15 carts with documented utilization rates and maintenance records proves recurring revenue. Community presence — Endorsements from HOA communities, partnerships with local media, and a strong Google review profile are intangible assets that a buyer can step into. Clean financials — At minimum, two to three years of tax returns, bank statements, and a profit-and-loss statement that a CPA has touched. Documented processes — Even simple things: a written list of how you price carts, where you source inventory, what your rental rates are by season, and who your repeat customers are.

Why Option A is Usually the Best Play

Strategic buyers — other dealers, powersports businesses, or even rental companies — will typically pay the highest premium because they see synergies you can't capture alone. They're not just buying your cash flow; they're buying your customer list, your community endorsements, and elimination of a local competitor. In a growing market like Northeast Florida's golf cart-friendly communities, that has real value.

04 — Buyer Psychology

What Buyers Actually Care About

Understanding what's going through a buyer's head helps you prepare the right things and avoid wasting time on the wrong things.

The Five Questions Every Buyer Asks

1. "Can I make my money back?" — This is the earnings question. They want to see that the business will pay for itself within 2-4 years. If your SDE supports a comfortable payback window at the asking price, you pass the first filter.

2. "Will it keep working without the current owner?" — This is the transferability question, and it's the one that kills most small dealer deals. If every customer relationship, every vendor call, and every rental booking runs through you personally, the buyer is taking a huge risk that revenue walks out the door when you do.

3. "Is this growing or declining?" — Buyers want to see a trend line moving in the right direction. If you've gone from 60 units to 100+ units in a couple of years, that's a powerful story. If rental revenue has been climbing, even better. Document that trajectory.

4. "What are the risks?" — Manufacturer relationship stability, lease terms, local regulation changes on LSVs, seasonal revenue fluctuations, competition entering the market. The more openly you address these, the more credible you are.

5. "Can I improve it?" — Smart buyers want upside. If you can show that there's untapped potential (online sales, expanded service offerings, fleet rental contracts with resorts or communities), you're giving them a reason to pay more today for what they can build tomorrow.

05 — The Roadmap

A 12-Month Exit Preparation Plan

You don't need to hire a team or overhaul everything. But spending 12 months getting your house in order can meaningfully increase what someone will pay.

Months 1-3 — Foundation
Get the Numbers Clean
Have a CPA review your financials and produce a clear profit-and-loss statement. Separate personal and business expenses. Document your actual SDE. Start tracking rental revenue and unit sales in a simple spreadsheet, at minimum. This is the single highest-ROI activity for increasing sale price.
Months 3-6 — Documentation
Write Down What's in Your Head
Create a simple operations manual: how you source carts, your pricing strategy, your rental process, seasonal patterns, your key customer contacts, and your vendor/supplier relationships. This doesn't need to be fancy. Even a Google Doc with 10-15 pages covering the basics is transformative for a buyer's confidence.
Months 4-8 — Systems
Move Key Processes Off Paper
Digitize your rental booking system (even a simple calendar tool), build a basic customer contact list, and start collecting Google reviews systematically. The goal isn't to become a tech company — it's to create records that a new owner can pick up and use on day one.
Months 6-9 — Positioning
Build the Growth Story
Document your sales trajectory. If you've gone from 60 to 100+ units, show it. Identify and write down specific growth opportunities you haven't pursued yet: resort fleet contracts, online sales expansion, service add-ons, new communities being built nearby. These become the "upside narrative" that gets buyers excited.
Months 9-12 — Go to Market
Find Your Buyer
Engage a business broker (they typically charge 8-12% of sale price), or start conversations directly with potential strategic buyers. Prepare a one-page summary of the business with key metrics. Have your CPA or a valuation professional give you a defensible asking price. Begin confidential conversations with interested parties.
The Rule of Thumb

Every dollar you invest in documentation and systems during this 12-month window will likely return $3-5 in sale price, because you're shifting the buyer's perception from "I'm buying a guy's hustle" to "I'm buying a real business." That perception shift is where the multiple moves from 1.5x to 2.5x or higher.

07 — How Deals Work

Understanding Deal Structure

Most small business sales don't happen as a single check. Understanding how deals are typically structured helps you plan for what you'll actually receive and when.

Seller Financing Is Normal

In the vast majority of small business sales, the buyer doesn't pay the full price upfront. A typical structure might look like 30-50% down payment at closing, with the remaining balance paid over 3-5 years through seller financing. This isn't a red flag; it's how the market works. SBA loans can sometimes cover the buyer's portion, but the seller should expect to carry some paper.

Transition Period

Plan on staying involved for 60-90 days minimum after closing. Some deals include a consulting agreement where you're paid separately for 6-12 months of advisory support. This is especially important in a relationship-driven business where customers know you by name and your vendor contacts need to be transitioned personally.

Asset Sale vs. Entity Sale

Most small business purchases are structured as asset sales, where the buyer purchases the inventory, equipment, customer lists, and goodwill — but not the legal entity itself. This is generally cleaner and easier for both parties. Your attorney and CPA should advise on which structure best serves your tax situation.

Non-Compete Agreement

Expect the buyer to require a non-compete clause, typically 2-5 years within a defined geographic radius. This is standard and reasonable. A buyer won't pay full value if you can open a competing shop down the road six months later.

09 — Final Thought

The Difference Between Selling a Job and Selling a Business

"A business that can run without you is worth multiples more than one that can't. The exit starts not when you list it for sale, but when you start building something a stranger could walk into and operate."

Everything in this guide comes back to one idea: transferability. The golf cart market in Northeast Florida is strong and growing. The communities being built, the shift toward electric transportation, the lifestyle demand — all of it favors a well-run dealer. The question isn't whether there's a buyer out there. The question is whether what you've built can be handed off in a way that makes a buyer confident enough to write the check.

The good news is that most of the work to get there isn't expensive or complicated. It's documentation, a few simple systems, and the discipline to track what's already happening in a way someone else can read. Twelve months of that work can be the difference between liquidating inventory and walking away with real value for what you've built.

Next Steps

This guide is a starting point. The logical next moves are: (1) get your financials reviewed by a CPA who has experience with small business sales, (2) start documenting your operations with even a simple written manual, and (3) digitize your rental and customer records so there's a trail a buyer can see. Those three actions alone will put you ahead of 90% of small business owners thinking about an exit.
